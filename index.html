Pixelator

By Isabelle Lee

I. Description

Pixelator turns images into arrays of integers that represent pixel data. There are four
numbers for each pixel denoting red, green, blue, and alpha (opacity) values that
range from 0 to 255.

I chose a set of 200 pixels by 200 pixels images that the user could choose from.
Because this would yield a total of 40,000 pixels and 4 data points for each, which
would result in an array with 160,000 elements, I chose to average the values of
100 pixel regions after separating them into their respective red, green, blue, and
alpha arrays. This resulted in 4 arrays of length 400 each.

I then fed these arrays into different oscillators and filters to produce and
manipulate the sound. I attached frequency of the sound to the array of red pixel data,
and frequency would increase for regions with more red. I attached the resonance, or
brightness, of the sound to the array of blue pixel data. I fed it into the parameter 'Q'
for a biquad filter. I attached the tremolo the sound to the array of green pixel data,
and attributed increased intensity of tremolo to increased concentration of green in the region.
I unfortuantely could not do much with the alpha value, because all my images were uniform
in opacity, and it would not lead to useful nor interesting manipulations of sound.

II. Images

I chose to use images from the Impressionist and Post-Impressionist period of art, spanning from
the mid-1800s to early 1900s. I always found art from this period very fascinating, and chose
famous works from three famous artists - Piet Mondrian, George Seurat, and Vincent van Gogh.

The first button, "Galaxy", is just a generic image I used for testing the early stages of
this project. It had a simple blend of colors so I could track if changes were being made
in the sound correctly.

Piet Mondrian's distinct artistic style was one that valued geometric shapes, overlooked
natural form and color, and mainly used straight lines to form rectangular areas. When his
painting is put through the Pixelator, notice that it sounds much more robotic and jagged
compared to the other images, due its sharp borders and abrupt color changes.

George Seurat's style is softer and much more blended. The chosen work of art A Sunday on La
Grande Jatte (1884), is an example of style he pioneered called pointilism. When zoomed in,
one can see that the image is made up of small dots. The sound produced by this image is varied
because there is a diverse pallette of color.

Vincent van Gogh's style has changed throughout his life time. I chose one of his most well-known
works of art The Starry Night
